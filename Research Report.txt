1.Myanmar Snub-nosed Monkey
  

Scientific Name
Rhinopithecus strykeri
Conservation Status
Critically Endangered (CR) according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature Red List.
Geographic Distribution
The Myanmar snub-nosed monkey is endemic to northern Myanmar, meaning it is found nowhere else in the world. Its known range is mainly in the mountainous forests of Kachin State, close to the Myanmar–China border. Small populations may also occur in adjacent areas of southwestern China.
Habitat
This species inhabits temperate broadleaf and mixed conifer forests at elevations between 1,700 and 3,200 metres above sea level. These forests provide a cool climate and abundant food resources such as leaves, fruits, lichens, and seeds. The monkeys spend almost their entire lives in the forest canopy, where they travel, feed, and rest.
Major Threats
* Habitat destruction caused by commercial logging and agricultural expansion.
* Forest fragmentation, which isolates small populations and limits breeding opportunities.
* Illegal hunting for bushmeat and local wildlife trade.
* Road construction and infrastructure development that open remote forests to human activities.
* Climate change, which may alter the high-altitude forests the species depends on.
Human Activities Responsible
* Unsustainable timber extraction.
* Expansion of farmland and shifting cultivation into forested areas.
* Construction of roads and settlements in previously undisturbed forests.
* Illegal hunting and wildlife trafficking.
* Weak enforcement of environmental protection laws in remote regions.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
The Myanmar snub-nosed monkey is an important seed disperser. By consuming fruits and depositing seeds throughout the forest, it helps maintain plant diversity and supports natural forest regeneration.
A continued decline in its population could lead to:
* Reduced regeneration of native tree species.
* Lower biodiversity within mountain forest ecosystems.
* Disruption of ecological interactions between plants and animals.
* Greater vulnerability of the ecosystem to environmental disturbances, reducing its long-term stability.
Future Outlook
The future of this species remains uncertain. Although conservation programmes and protected areas have improved monitoring and habitat protection, the population is still extremely small and fragmented. Without stronger law enforcement, sustainable forest management, and continued community involvement, the species faces a high risk of extinction within the coming decades. However, if current conservation efforts are expanded and illegal hunting is effectively controlled, the population has the potential to recover gradually.
Ways to Save the Species
* Expand and strengthen protected forest reserves in northern Myanmar.
* Enforce stricter laws against illegal logging and wildlife hunting.
* Restore degraded forest habitats through reforestation programmes.
* Develop sustainable livelihoods for local communities to reduce dependence on forest resources.
* Support scientific research and long-term population monitoring.
* Increase public awareness about the importance of conserving Myanmar's endemic wildlife.
* Promote international cooperation between Myanmar and China to protect cross-border habitats.

2. Asian Elephant   


Scientific Name
Elephas maximus
Conservation Status
Endangered (EN)
Geographic Distribution
Asian elephants are found in Myanmar, India, Sri Lanka, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Bhutan, Nepal, Bangladesh, and China. Myanmar supports one of the largest remaining wild populations in Southeast Asia.
Habitat
They inhabit tropical and subtropical forests, grasslands, bamboo forests, and river valleys. Elephants require extensive habitats with reliable water sources and abundant vegetation.
Major Threats
* Habitat loss and fragmentation
* Illegal poaching for ivory and skin
* Human-elephant conflict
* Infrastructure development
Human Activities Responsible
Rapid agricultural expansion, logging, mining, road construction, and urban development reduce available habitat. Farmers may kill elephants that damage crops, while poachers target males for their tusks.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
Asian elephants are known as ecosystem engineers. They disperse seeds over long distances, create forest clearings that encourage plant diversity, and dig water holes that benefit many other species. Their decline reduces biodiversity and alters forest structure.
Future Outlook
Although protected under national laws and international agreements, elephant populations continue to decline in many regions. Long-term survival depends on preserving connected habitats and reducing conflicts with local communities.
Ways to Save the Species
* Expand protected forest corridors.
* Improve anti-poaching enforcement.
* Promote elephant-friendly farming methods.
* Develop early-warning systems to reduce human-elephant conflict.
* Educate local communities about elephant conservation.

7. Sun Bear   
Scientific Name
Helarctos malayanus
Conservation Status
Vulnerable (VU)
Geographic Distribution
The sun bear is distributed across Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei, and parts of southern China. In Myanmar, it is mainly found in evergreen and mixed deciduous forests.
Habitat
Sun bears inhabit lowland tropical rainforests, evergreen forests, and mixed deciduous forests. They are excellent climbers and spend much of their time in trees, where they build nests for resting and feeding.
Major Threats
* Deforestation and habitat fragmentation
* Illegal hunting for traditional medicine
* Illegal pet trade
* Declining food resources
Human Activities Responsible
Commercial logging, agricultural expansion, mining, and road construction destroy forest habitats. Bears are also hunted for their gall bladders, paws, and cubs, which are sold in illegal wildlife markets.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
Sun bears help maintain healthy forests by dispersing seeds and controlling insect populations. They also create tree cavities while searching for insects, which later become nesting sites for birds and small mammals.
Future Outlook
The species is expected to continue declining if illegal wildlife trade and forest destruction are not effectively controlled. Stronger conservation policies could stabilize populations.
Ways to Save the Species
* Strengthen anti-poaching patrols.
* Protect remaining rainforest habitats.
* Enforce bans on illegal wildlife trade.
* Educate communities about wildlife conservation.
* Expand protected forest areas.

12. Gaur   
Scientific Name
Bos gaurus
Conservation Status
Vulnerable (VU)
Geographic Distribution
The gaur is found in Myanmar, India, Nepal, Bhutan, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam, Malaysia, and China. Myanmar contains some of the largest remaining populations in Southeast Asia.
Habitat
Gaurs inhabit evergreen forests, moist deciduous forests, and open grasslands close to permanent water sources. They prefer large, undisturbed landscapes with abundant grazing vegetation.
Major Threats
* Habitat destruction
* Illegal hunting
* Competition with livestock
* Disease transmission from domestic cattle
Human Activities Responsible
Forest conversion for agriculture, logging, cattle grazing, and infrastructure development reduce available habitat. Diseases such as foot-and-mouth disease can spread from domestic cattle to wild gaur populations.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
As one of Asia's largest herbivores, gaurs shape vegetation by grazing and browsing. Their movement also disperses seeds and creates pathways used by smaller wildlife. Their decline alters plant communities and reduces food availability for predators such as tigers.
Future Outlook
With stronger habitat protection and disease management, gaur populations could remain stable in protected areas. However, habitat fragmentation remains a long-term concern.
Ways to Save the Species
* Protect large forest reserves.
* Reduce livestock grazing in protected areas.
* Vaccinate domestic cattle.
* Strengthen anti-poaching patrols.
* Restore degraded forests.

21. Amur Leopard  
Scientific Name
Panthera pardus orientalis
Conservation Status
Critically Endangered (CR)
Geographic Distribution
Amur leopards are native to the border region between the Russian Far East and northeast China. Their wild distribution is restricted to a narrow strip along the Amur River basin.
Habitat
They inhabit temperate deciduous and mixed broadleaf forests, rocky mountainsides, and high-altitude valleys, adapting well to freezing snowy winters.
Major Threats
* Severe poaching for their beautiful spotted fur coats
* Deforestation from commercial logging and forest fires
* Decline in natural prey species like deer and wild boar
Human Activities Responsible
Illegal loggers and agricultural clearings shrink their dense woodland territories, while illegal poachers deliberately hunt them for the black market fur trade. Additionally, humans overhunt local deer populations, leaving the leopards with a critically low food supply.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
As apex predators, Amur leopards play a central role in regulating the health and population numbers of herbivores. Their decline causes an overpopulation of deer and small mammals, which can lead to overgrazing, destroying forest undergrowth and affecting forest regeneration.
Future Outlook
With only around 100 to 120 individuals remaining in the wild, they are one of the rarest big cats on Earth. While intensive conservation efforts in Russia's Land of the Leopard National Park have stabilized their numbers, their extremely narrow genetic pool makes them vulnerable to disease.
Ways to Save the Species
* Expand and strictly monitor transboundary wildlife corridors between Russia and China.
* Impose maximum criminal penalties for poaching and trafficking leopard parts.
* Implement managed prey restoration programs to boost deer populations.
* Deploy advanced anti-poaching camera traps and satellite monitoring.

22. Sumatran Orangutan  
Scientific Name
Pongo abelii
Conservation Status
Critically Endangered (CR)
Geographic Distribution
They are completely endemic to the island of Sumatra in Indonesia, with the vast majority of the remaining wild populations restricted to the northern Leuser Ecosystem.
Habitat
They live almost exclusively in lowland tropical rainforests, peat swamp forests, and mountain valleys, spending over 95% of their lives high up in the tree canopy.
Major Threats
* Massive habitat fragmentation due to palm oil production
* Construction of roads cutting through protected reserves
* Illegal capture of infants for the domestic and international pet trade
Human Activities Responsible
Large agricultural corporations set illegal forest fires and clear vast expanses of ancient jungle to establish commercial oil palm plantations. When mother orangutans wander into farms looking for food, humans often kill them and capture their babies to sell into the black-market exotic pet trade.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
Orangutans are known as the "gardeners of the forest." They eat hundreds of different types of fruits and pass the large seeds completely intact across miles of jungle. Without them, many large-seeded tropical tree species would fail to reproduce, severely changing the forest's structure.
Future Outlook
Their populations continue to drop sharply as their forests are sliced apart by development. Because female orangutans only give birth once every 8 to 9 years, their natural recovery rate is incredibly slow, putting them at extreme risk of extinction if logging continues.
Ways to Save the Species
* Enforce strict bans on clearing pristine rainforests for commercial palm oil.
* Purchase and dynamically restore degraded land to create connected forest bridges.
* Support rescue centers that rehabilitate and reintroduce confiscated pet orangutans.
* Encourage consumers to buy only certified sustainable palm oil products.

23. Mountain Gorilla  
Scientific Name
Gorilla beringei beringei
Conservation Status
Endangered (EN)
Geographic Distribution
Mountain gorillas are found in two isolated populations in Central Africa: the Virunga volcanic mountain range (spanning Rwanda, Uganda, and the Democratic Republic of Congo) and Uganda’s Bwindi Impenetrable National Park.
Habitat
They live in high-altitude, dense tropical mist forests and subalpine woodlands, operating at elevations ranging from 8,000 to 13,000 feet.
Major Threats
* Close proximity to human settlements leading to disease transmission
* Accidental trapping in snares set for other bushmeat animals
* Habitat encroachment by growing local communities
Human Activities Responsible
As human populations expand around the park borders, forests are cleared for farming and livestock grazing. Civil unrest in the region pushes refugees into the parks for charcoal production, and gorillas are frequently injured or killed by wire snares set by local poachers targeting bush pigs and antelopes.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
Mountain gorillas are massive herbivores that consume massive quantities of celery, bamboo, and foliage every single day. Their heavy grazing acts as a natural pruning system, opening up paths in the thick vegetation so that lower-lying plants receive sunlight to grow.
Future Outlook
Thanks to intense, community-led "eco-tourism" and dedicated veterinary teams, mountain gorillas are one of the few great ape populations showing a steady increase, now numbering over 1,000 individuals. However, they remain highly vulnerable to human respiratory illnesses due to shared genetics.
Ways to Save the Species
* Train and equip local park rangers to locate and remove wire and rope snares.
* Limit the size of tourist groups and enforce strict mask-wearing rules to prevent passing diseases to the gorillas.
* Share eco-tourism revenue directly with local villages to build schools and hospitals, incentivizing protection.
* Implement community-based agricultural programs outside park boundaries to stop forest encroachment.

24. Sunda Tiger (Sumatran Tiger)  
Scientific Name
Panthera tigris sumatrae
Conservation Status
Critically Endangered (CR)
Geographic Distribution
They are the last surviving island tiger species in the world, found solely on the island of Sumatra, Indonesia.
Habitat
They are highly adaptable, living in coastal mangrove swamps, lowland river basin forests, and high-altitude mountain rainforests.
Major Threats
* Heavy poaching for bones and skins used in traditional items
* Rapid habitat destruction from paper pulp and palm oil logging
* Escalating human-wildlife conflict near agricultural boundaries
Human Activities Responsible
Poachers set hidden wire loops to deliberately trap and kill tigers for profit. As timber companies destroy their primary hunting grounds, hungry tigers venture into human settlements, leading to retaliatory killings by villagers defending their livestock and families.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
As top-tier apex predators, Sunda tigers keep populations of wild boars, deer, and macaques in check. If the tigers disappear, an explosion in prey populations will cause intense over-grazing, destroying the native flora and disrupting the balance of smaller forest animals.
Future Outlook

Ways to Save the Species

* Perform routine medical testing and "chelation therapy" to remove lead from wild condors.
* Organize community trash clean-ups in condor nesting and foraging zones.
* Modify and insulate power grid lines to prevent massive birds from getting electrocuted.

 Open grasslands, savannas, semi-deserts, and dry plains.
Major Threats
* Habitat loss
* Illegal hunting
* Egg collection
* Climate change
* Human disturbance
Human Activities Responsible
 Agricultural expansion, hunting, and habitat destruction reduce wild ostrich populations in some regions.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
 Ostriches disperse seeds and help maintain healthy grassland ecosystems. Their decline can reduce biodiversity and affect ecosystem balance.
Future Outlook
 Wild populations remain stable overall, but local conservation is needed where habitat loss is increasing.
Ways to Save the Species
* Protect grassland habitats.
* Prevent illegal hunting.
* Restore degraded ecosystems.
* Support sustainable land management.
* Raise public awareness about wildlife conservation.

1. Dugong
  

Scientific Name
Dugong dugon
Conservation Status
Vulnerable (VU)
Geographic Distribution
The dugong is found in the warm coastal waters of the Indian Ocean and western Pacific Ocean, including the Andaman Sea, the Bay of Bengal, northern Australia, the Red Sea, and Southeast Asia. Small populations occur along Myanmar's coastline.
Habitat
Dugongs inhabit shallow coastal waters, seagrass meadows, lagoons, and sheltered bays. They depend almost entirely on healthy seagrass beds for food.
Major Threats
* Loss of seagrass habitats
* Accidental capture in fishing nets (bycatch)
* Boat strikes
* Coastal pollution
* Climate change
Human Activities Responsible
Coastal development, destructive fishing practices, pollution from agriculture and industry, and increased boat traffic degrade seagrass ecosystems and increase mortality.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
Dugongs maintain healthy seagrass meadows through grazing. Their decline can lead to overgrown seagrass, reduced biodiversity, and less productive coastal ecosystems that support fish and other marine life.
Future Outlook
Without stronger protection of seagrass habitats and sustainable fisheries management, dugong populations are expected to continue declining in many parts of their range.
Ways to Save the Species
* Protect seagrass meadows.
* Reduce bycatch through improved fishing gear.
* Establish marine protected areas.
* Control coastal pollution.
* Monitor populations through scientific research.

Geographic Distribution

Open oceans, coastal waters, and tropical nesting beaches.
Major Threats
* Plastic pollution
* Longline fishing
* Climate change
* Coastal development
* Egg harvesting
Human Activities Responsible
Plastic bags are often mistaken for jellyfish, causing fatal blockages when ingested. Fishing gear entanglement and coastal development also threaten survival.
Ecological Consequences of Decline
Leatherback turtles consume large quantities of jellyfish, helping regulate jellyfish populations. Their decline may contribute to jellyfish blooms, which can negatively affect fish populations and commercial fisheries.
Future Outlook
The species will continue declining unless global cooperation improves protection of nesting beaches and reduces marine pollution.
Ways to Save the Species
* Reduce single-use plastics.
* Use turtle-safe fishing gear.
* Protect nesting beaches.
* Restore coastal ecosystems.
* Support international marine conservation programmes.
